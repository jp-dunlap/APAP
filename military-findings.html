Military, Security & Law Enforcement Ties

The Arkansas Nexus: Exporting and Importing the Tools of Repression

Report Overview: The Symbiotic Loop

This investigation reveals a symbiotic relationship in Arkansas between its actively promoted defense-industrial complex and the increasing militarization of its domestic law enforcement agencies. This dynamic facilitates both the export of instruments of control—weaponry, surveillance technology, and advanced munitions—to global conflict zones, and the import of militarized tactics, equipment, and ideologies into Arkansas's own policing landscape. This report deconstructs this cycle, examining how the methodologies of control are both disseminated from and adopted within the state.

Forging the Arsenal: Arkansas's State-Sanctioned Defense Industry

The Political Economy of Defense in Arkansas

The aerospace and defense (A&D) sector is actively cultivated by the state as a core component of its economy. The Arkansas Economic Development Commission (AEDC) champions the industry, which has become the state's leading export category.

The Militarization of Arkansas Police

Analytical Framework: The "Deadly Exchange"A term from a 2018 report by Jewish Voice for Peace, critiquing U.S. law enforcement training programs with Israeli forces for allegedly importing tactics of surveillance, racial profiling, and suppression of dissent developed in the context of military occupation. Paradigm

While the report finds no direct evidence of Arkansas State Police or North Little Rock Police participating in specific ADL- or JINSA-sponsored trainings in Israel, the "Deadly Exchange" framework is useful for analyzing the importation of militarized tactics through other channels, such as national policing trends, federal grant programs, and domestic training that incorporates Israeli-style "best practices."

Profile of Militarized Capabilities in Arkansas Law Enforcement

Key state and local police forces exhibit significant investment in specialized units, training, and equipment that align with broader national trends toward police militarization.

Law Enforcement Agency Specialized Units Key Training Areas Notable Equipment

The formal structures within these agencies—SWAT teams, Fusion Centers, and drone units—signify a preparedness for, and normalization of, paramilitary responses to domestic situations. The NLRPD, for example, reported 154 "Response to Resistance" (use of force) incidents in 2024 alone.

The Symbiotic Loop: Reinforcing Repression

Economic Imperatives & Political Cover

The defense industry's significant economic contribution provides powerful political incentives for state officials to protect and champion it. Governor Sanders' reference to Arkansas as "America's arsenal" provides political cover for an industry whose products are inherently tied to conflict. This creates a self-reinforcing loop: economic benefits drive military production, which normalizes militarized solutions and facilitates their adoption by domestic law enforcement.

From Global Theaters to Local Streets: The Feedback Mechanism

Technologies honed for export in external conflict zones become increasingly available for domestic application. The "dual-use" nature of surveillance systems, drones, and armored vehicles is a key enabler. While direct sales from Arkansas defense plants to Arkansas police are not documented, the local production lowers political and psychological barriers for police to acquire similar technologies via federal programs like the 1033 ProgramA U.S. Department of Defense program that transfers surplus military equipment, including armored vehicles, rifles, and aircraft, to civilian law enforcement agencies, often at little to no cost.. The logic of control embedded in these technologies is thus imported into domestic policing.

Conclusion & Recommendations

Summary of Findings

This investigation demonstrates a symbiotic relationship within Arkansas where the state's promotion of its defense export industry creates an environment conducive to the militarization of its own police forces. This cycle, where the tools and logics of control are both exported globally and reflected domestically, has profound implications for democratic accountability, human rights, and racial justice within the state.

Pathways for Demilitarization and Accountability

1. Enhance Transparency & Accountability

  • Implement state-level reviews of the human rights implications of military hardware exported from Arkansas.
  • Establish and empower robust, independent civilian oversight boards with authority to review police acquisition of military equipment and use-of-force policies.

2. Scrutinize and Limit Police Militarization

  • Conduct thorough audits on the necessity and impact of militarized police units like SWAT and ERT.
  • Implement clear restrictions on the use of military-grade equipment in routine policing and against protestors, prioritizing de-escalation.

3. Promote Alternative Economic Development

  • Invest in diverse economic strategies not reliant on the defense industry, such as renewable energy, public health, and sustainable civilian manufacturing.

4. Foster Critical Public Dialogue

  • Support independent research and public forums on the impact of the defense industry and police militarization on Arkansas communities.
