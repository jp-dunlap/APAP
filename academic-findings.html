Academic & Institutional Ties

Case Study: The University of Arkansas System

Report Overview & Framing

This report investigates the multifaceted ways the University of Arkansas system is complicit in practices and policies constituting what numerous international human rights organizations (Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch) and legal scholars term the "Israeli apartheid project." The investigation examines financial investments, academic partnerships, adherence to state legislation, and the prevailing campus climate. Institutional complicity is defined as actions or omissions that aid, facilitate, or legitimize this system, occurring through financial, academic, and policy channels.

Tracing University Funds to the Israeli Apartheid Project

The UA Foundation, with over $2 billion in assets, engages external investment managers who direct endowment funds into the global market. An analysis of the public filings of these managers reveals significant holdings in companies with documented ties to the Israeli military, surveillance apparatus, and settlement economy.

Institutional Policies and Partnerships

Academic Partnership: The University of Haifa

The UArk system's past study abroad partnership with the University of Haifa represents a direct academic link to an institution deeply integrated with the Israeli military and security state.

  • Haifa's National Security Studies M.A. was developed in partnership with Israel's National Security College, the IDF's highest course of study.
  • The IDF's Tactical Command College incorporates a Bachelor's degree from Haifa University into its training.
  • The prestigious Israel National Defense College includes an M.A. from Haifa University in its program for senior military and government officials.

This partnership lends academic legitimacy to an institution that plays a direct role in training the security forces of a state accused of apartheid, raising serious questions about the university's human rights due diligence.

The Legislative Shield: State Laws Compelling Complicity

Arkansas state laws create a powerful external pressure on the University system, restricting its ethical autonomy and compelling complicity.

  • Act 710 (Anti-BDS Law): Legally prevents the University from divesting from companies involved in the Israeli occupation if such an act is deemed a "boycott," forcing continued financial entanglement. This law was cited in the cancellation of a talk by author Nathan Thrall at UA Fayetteville in 2023.
  • Act 126 (IHRA Definition): Provides a state-sanctioned tool to suppress criticism of Israeli policies by equating it with antisemitism, thereby chilling academic freedom and dissent on campus.

Governance: Board of Trustees' Inaction

A review of the UA Board of Trustees meeting minutes from 2023-2024 reveals a significant pattern: a lack of any explicit public discussion or recorded votes on divestment from companies complicit in the occupation, the ethical implications of the Haifa partnership, or the impact of Acts 710 and 126 on campus. This public silence allows potentially problematic entanglements to persist unexamined at the system's highest governance tier.

Campus Climate and the Suppression of Dissent

The "Palestine Exception" to Free Speech

The UArk system operates within a national context of widespread suppression of speech supporting Palestinian rights. Act 126 provides a legal framework that institutionalizes this "Palestine exception" in Arkansas.

Palestine Legal reported a 600% increase in requests for legal support between 2022 and 2024, with two-thirds of cases being campus-related. Tactics include disciplinary actions, censorship of events, and public condemnations of Palestine advocacy by university administrations.

External Influence Networks

  • Hillel International: The national leadership of this major Jewish campus organization has strong, documented pro-Israel advocacy credentials. Its chair, Lee Dranikoff, co-founded the Brand Israel Group and helped draft the initial strategy for the Israel on Campus Coalition (ICC). This orientation can contribute to a campus climate where Palestine solidarity activism is marginalized.
  • Canary MissionAn anonymous, online blacklisting operation that publishes dossiers on students and academics critical of Israeli policies, aiming to damage their reputations and future employment prospects.: This anonymous blacklisting website actively creates a climate of fear. It publishes dossiers on pro-Palestine advocates, aiming to intimidate them into silence. The existence of such sites, combined with Act 126, raises the stakes for any student or faculty member at UArk engaging in this discourse.

Conclusion & Recommendations for Action

Summary of Findings

The University of Arkansas system's complicity is not isolated but is woven through its financial architecture, academic engagements, and policy adherence. "Business as usual," operating within the existing legal and political framework, results in de facto material and symbolic support for the Israeli state apparatus and policies widely condemned as constituting apartheid.

Recommendations for Accountability

1. Financial Transparency & Divestment

  • Demand full public disclosure of all UA Foundation investment holdings.
  • Call for immediate divestment from companies identified as complicit in human rights violations in Palestine (e.g., Microsoft, Google, RTX, Lockheed Martin).

2. Legislative & Policy Reform

  • Support efforts to repeal or reform Arkansas Acts 710 and 126 to protect free speech and ethical governance.
  • Demand the University establish robust ethical guidelines for all academic partnerships and corporate donations, including human rights due diligence.

3. Protect Academic Freedom

  • Urge the UArk administration to issue a clear statement affirming the right to criticize Israeli policies and support BDS.
  • Demand the University reject the weaponization of the IHRA definition to silence legitimate political speech.

4. Engage University Governance

  • Organize students, faculty, and alumni to present these findings directly to the Board of Trustees.
  • Utilize public comment periods at Board meetings to demand accountability and action on these issues.
